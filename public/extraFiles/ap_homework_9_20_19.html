What we are going to discuss on Friday are principles regarding forces and motion that are highly counterintuitive. Something is counterintuitive when it seems to be very simple and to make sense right away, but on closer examination the idea you thought was true is incorrect! In particular, we are going to examine the highly counterintuitive idea that force does not cause motion, it cases changes in motion.

Re-read chapter 15 and 16, of The Clockwork Universe by Edward Dolnick. (pages 90 - 102). [They are called A Play Without and Audience and All in Pieces. This chapter discusses how early physicists such as Newton and Galileo disproved ideas that had been seen as common sense, obvious facts for centuries. Answer the following questions:

  1. The ancient Greek philosophers, such as Aristotle, pointed out differences between two different universes: the cosmos, and here on earth. What were the differences they saw?
  2. How did Galileo's approach differ from Aristotle's?
  3. What Polish thinker first posited that the earth is is constant motion?
  4. Copernicus's model faced two major hurdles: it seemd to violate religious ideas and it seemed to violate common sense. Focus on the commonsense arguments against the model that the earth moves. What were they?
  5. In order to explain the perfect, unending motion of planets, the Ancient Greeks devised a system in which planets moved in circles inside of circles. Why did they need to do this? Why didn't the planets just move around in a circle?
  6. What are the mathematical advantages of Copernicus's heliocentric theory over the Ancient Greek's circles inside of circles theory?
